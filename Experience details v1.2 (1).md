

## **English Translation — Work Experience Details**

### **Joybuy**

I built a Power BI tool that enabled the manager to track the sales performance of specific SKUs, assigned product managers, and weekly campaigns. Overall, the FMCG team consisted of three managers.

I also developed an AI-assisted tool that learned sales patterns based on historical product prices and units sold in previous months. The tool could forecast how many units were likely to be sold over the next four weeks at target prices defined by product managers, assuming a 10% weekly growth rate. After real-world testing, the tool achieved up to 80% accuracy and provided reliable insights for the FMCG team.

I supported the product onboarding process by making sure items were listed correctly. I communicated with suppliers and coordinated with them whenever we needed additional or updated data.

We also maintained strong communication with the operations management team in China, which helped us identify and resolve issues faster.

Every week, I fully designed and updated landing pages for special sales campaigns across the FMCG and Local Food teams. This required high attention to detail.

I calculated the real weekly profit based on purchase price, selling price, units sold, and supplier rebates. Because of this, we always needed very accurate calculations to understand the actual weekly profit.

I was also the main contact point with the marketing team. When any member of our team was on leave, I handled updates related to discounted products, discount levels, and any information the marketing team needed.

I managed and built special campaign landing pages for different marketing promotions, from implementation and design to weekly product updates.

#### **Additional Details**

Each week, I prepared performance reports showing which SKUs performed best, which campaigns had the highest ROI, and which product managers delivered the strongest results.

I checked competitor prices and campaigns weekly and compared them with our own website. Upcoming competitor campaigns could be found on allefolders.nl, which I reviewed regularly. Then, using Claude AI, I collected competitors’ promotion links and product details into an Excel file to help category managers process the information more efficiently.

I was also involved in forecasting potential out-of-stock situations. I predicted how many units we were likely to sell in upcoming promotions, and based on current inventory, we could estimate how many additional units were needed. I handled the calculation side, while follow-up and operational support were managed with the China team.

When sales data came from China or suppliers, I cleaned the data myself, mostly in Excel, because data transfer between employees was very important for us. I built Microsoft Excel tools that could update the entire file and formulas with one click.

The FMCG category had around 5,000 SKUs.

I managed communication with 8 suppliers.

The forecasting tool was tested every week because promotions were weekly and accurate estimates were important. On average, around 350 products were forecasted each week.

The dashboard was mainly used by the FMCG team lead, but managers for household, personal care, and pet/baby categories also used it. In total, around four managers used the dashboard.

I updated 8 landing pages per week. With the help of AI, these pages required translation into four languages: Dutch, English, French, and Chinese. They also needed to be prepared for both mobile and desktop.

On average, each landing page included around 350 products from our team and 300 products from the Local Food team.

I handled rebate calculations for the five largest suppliers in the team. These rebates were based on weekly promotions for specific EANs and needed to be calculated monthly. I cannot share exact figures because they are confidential company information.

### **Versuni**

I was responsible for calculating the monthly affiliate partner share that Philips needed to pay to partners based on their cooperation with us.

I also communicated directly with affiliate partners. For example, when we needed to understand their upcoming seasonal campaigns or special promotions, I coordinated with them and followed up internally with country sales managers.

I designed and built a Power BI dashboard that allowed our team to visually monitor partner performance. By selecting specific weeks or dates, we could quickly see campaigns we had already run or campaigns planned for the future. The dashboard also showed sales results and the costs paid to partners for previous campaigns.

I implemented several creative ideas there. For example, users referred from specific partner websites through UTM links could see different prices on the page. I used JavaScript through Google Tag Manager to check whether a specific URL parameter existed, and based on that parameter, the displayed product prices were adjusted with an additional discount. Users could then purchase products using the specific discount code assigned to each partner.

I also built an AI-assisted tool that generated customized JavaScript code. This allowed me to apply discounts to each product on a specific landing page based on the title above each product row, changing the displayed price dynamically.

#### **Additional Details**

I was involved in deciding which partner budgets should be increased or which partnerships should be reduced.

When working with GTM, I handled event tracking, UTM tracking, and conversion tracking.

I did not run formal A/B tests, but I made recommendations based on my previous experience.

I also worked on revenue attribution. At the end of each month, we had a list of partners and their assigned discount codes. By exporting discount-code usage data and linking each code to the relevant partner in an Excel pivot table, I could identify each partner’s performance.

The Power BI dashboard covered 17 partners.

The dashboard covered 8 European countries overall, although our main focus was the Benelux region. The team worked on European affiliate and loyalty programs with partners, and each partner could specialize in specific markets. The largest markets were Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, and France.

I am not sure how to measure exactly how many hours were saved, but without the tools I built, monthly reporting would take me around 3 full days. With the tools, I could prepare the reports in around 5 hours. Accuracy was very important because the correct file had to be shared with the correct partner.

The dynamic pricing idea was tested on the UK landing page during the final month of my internship. Customers could see the additional discount more clearly, especially when they had a discount code that applied on top of an existing 40% discount. Before that, users did not see the final price after applying their partner discount code on the affiliate landing page. Because my internship ended soon after the test, the effect on conversion or revenue was not measured.

In addition, for clients’ blog sections, which were usually built on WordPress, I developed SEO-focused plugins. For example, one plugin added blog schema markup to the page, and another plugin allowed FAQ sections to be added to blog posts while also inserting the related FAQ schema into the page HTML.

The company had a 10-person team: I was part of the marketing team, there were three developers, one UX designer, and two network support specialists.

Some clients needed keyword research and support with Google or Meta advertising, so as a member of the marketing team, I helped them launch their campaigns.

Using Google Analytics and Google Tag Manager, I built different tracking setups to measure KPIs such as how many users clicked the “add to cart” button, how many reached the checkout page, and how many users entered the website through different advertising campaigns such as Google Ads. More importantly, I measured user behavior quantitatively so I could provide better marketing and UX-related recommendations to our clients.

I also presented client performance after advertising campaigns. These reports included KPIs such as incoming users, campaign conversion rate, and user behavior on the website.

One of our older clients had a WordPress e-commerce website. I built a plugin for them that corrected products with duplicate attributes. This was a complex task because the website had 15,000 products, each with an average of 10 attributes, in the camera and photography equipment industry. Duplicate attributes needed to be removed or merged. This plugin was designed and implemented in one month and effectively replaced around four months of work by two full-time employees, without human error.

In the WordPress panel, the client could define how much the price should increase or decrease compared to the competitor’s OpenCart store. The plugin used a cron job to update the prices of 4,000 products twice per day.

For two specific clients, we ran Google Ads campaigns with a monthly budget of around €3,000 per client. We targeted keywords very precisely. The goal was not simply to generate the highest possible traffic; the goal was to achieve the highest conversion rate from ad visitors to customers. Sometimes, because the keywords were very specific, we intentionally limited visitor volume and had to run multiple campaigns.

### **19kala**

I built an internal tool where the PHP code, JavaScript, and database architecture were all designed by me. The tool was built mainly with PHP and allowed the SEO and content teams to follow up on all their tasks in one shared system.

For example, when the content team wanted to work on a specific topic, the task would be entered into the tool. Then, the SEO manager could approve or reject the topic in the next stage, add comments, reject it, give negative feedback, or give positive points for the suggestion. Each task passed through seven different stages until it was finally approved and published on the website.

This allowed us to see all files related to a task in one place. Work speed increased by up to 60%. Files were no longer lost, and all team members could see each other’s performance. Many managers strongly welcomed the tool. Within one year, 1,100 different website tasks were created and completed through the tool.

I also built a WordPress plugin to replace an old and heavy advertising plugin on the website. The plugin displayed specific banners and links in the blog based on campaigns defined in the plugin. It randomly showed them as a slider in the fifth paragraph of blog posts on the 19kala e-commerce blog. This allowed us to use blog visitors, around 60,000 users per day, to promote the store.

I built another tool to support the advertising display system. It automatically crawled products on special sale from the store section, which used OpenCart CMS, and selected 10 different products from different brands. It saved them and displayed them to blog users. This gave the company automatic ad updates in WordPress.

I supported both the e-commerce store and the WordPress blog to make sure technical SEO was handled correctly. For example, I checked page HTML tags, meta tags, and other SEO elements. At that time, AI was not powerful or available, so these checks had to be done manually. I was responsible for checking meta tags, correct HTML structure, and tagging across different page sections. If there was an issue, I created tickets for the OpenCart technical team and communicated with them until the issue was fixed. I handled the process from identifying the issue to following up on the solution.

I regularly joined SEO, content, and sales strategy meetings. We planned how to grow the website further. The most important KPI for us was organic traffic from Google, both for the store and the article section. Based on this, we planned strong strategies and decided how quickly, in what way, and in what order products should be added to the website for the best performance.

We also constantly analyzed keyword growth and ranking drops in Google Search Console to make sure traffic drops were not caused by our own actions. If there was anything we could do to prevent traffic loss, we did it.

Using PHP, I built a tool that crawled prices from different websites and showed us whether our mobile phone prices were competitive compared with other online stores selling the same products. The tool updated itself twice per day. Whenever competitors increased or decreased their prices and we no longer had the best or lowest market price, the tool sent an email to the sales team. This allowed them to update our prices as quickly as possible.

#### **Additional Details**

The task management tool did not only increase speed; it also reduced errors. We often had problems with lost keyword files for articles or products, or files being changed so we were never sure which version was the latest. We needed one tool to keep all data integrated in one place.

This project showed both my programming ability and my problem-solving ability. It was a major issue. During the first 10 days, I focused on the architecture of the tool: what we needed and how to solve the problem. Then I started designing the solution using my knowledge of databases, PHP, and JavaScript. The tool had to work on different devices such as laptops and mobile phones, and it had to give different users different access levels so they could or could not change a task at specific stages.

The tool significantly reduced errors. To understand the requirements, I had several meetings with employees from the SEO and content teams. Because I was the SEO manager, I could personally identify where the problems were and where we needed a tool to solve them. Unfortunately, AI tools were not available at that time; otherwise, they could have helped even more. I completed all the steps without AI support.

The SEO, content, and management teams all used the tool. The purpose was to remove the need for repeated follow-ups through phone calls and internal chat.

After building the tool, flawless content production capacity increased by 40%. Before the tool, we could publish around 15 articles and 30 new products per month. After the tool was built, this increased to 35 articles and 35 products per month.

The website had many technical SEO issues. By fixing them, the site experienced strong growth in a short period. Improvements to schema markup and site speed helped SEO significantly.

The biggest issue was that nobody had properly audited the website before. During my checks, we discovered that 70,000 incorrect links had been indexed by Google even though they were unnecessary and consuming crawl budget. We also found that CSS files were blocked for Google, meaning Google could not render the website correctly or understand the content properly. It was likely also affecting how Google evaluated the website’s mobile-friendliness.

I identified this issue and unblocked the CSS files. The site grew quickly, increasing from 30,000 to 190,000 daily users from Google within 9 months.

The price monitoring tool crawled prices for around 100 high-value mobile phone products from profitable brands such as Samsung, Apple, and Xiaomi. Price was very important for sales in this category. The tool updated its database three times per day. Whenever it detected that a competitor had reduced its price, or when no competitor had stock and we were the only store with availability, it sent an email notification to the sales team. The tool crawled five competitor websites selected by the sales team.

The SEO team had 5 members.

The content team had 10 members.

The task management tool had around 20 users, including the SEO team, content team, and managers.

The price monitoring tool checked 5 competitor stores.

It monitored around 100 main mobile phone products.

The advertising plugin was shown to blog visitors, sometimes reaching up to 100,000 visitors.

The traffic growth from 30,000 to 190,000 daily users was for the whole website. Usually, the blog accounted for around 60% to 70% of total website traffic.
